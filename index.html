In This Text Set

This text set includes informational texts, infographics, and a video. All deal with the ideal of equal voting rights and the steps taken to ensure those rights.

The Path to Voting Equality is the anchor text for this set. Depending on the needs of your class, you may choose to assign The Path to Voting Equality for independent reading, or you may choose to read it aloud with the class. Beginning with the anchor text will ground students in concepts and academic vocabulary that are key to discussing the Big Question. As students read the other texts in the set, they will compare their new understandings, as well as the structure and features of the text, with the anchor text.

After students read the anchor text, you may choose to assign specific texts or encourage students to choose the texts that appeal to them the most. Use the chart below to see where specific text structures and features appear across the texts in this set.

Discuss the Big Question

Before students begin reading the texts in this set, engage them in a class discussion around the Big Question.

Why is the right to vote important? How does the right to vote make it possible for people to change the world?

Have you seen news coverage of people protesting for certain rights? Why are rights worth fighting for?

What is something about society that you wish was different? Do you feel that you can make that change happen? Could you make a change if many more people were helping you?

The Path to Voting Equality

by Jody Kathryn
Interactive Timeline - Word Count: 576   Lexile: 960L

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Illustrate historical and contemporary means of changing society.

  • Content Objective: Students will understand key changes to voting rights since the 1700s.

  • Language Objective: Students will describe the series of events that lead to the United States’ current level of voting equality.

  • Text Structure Objective: Students will understand how a chronological text structure describes a series of events.

  • Text Feature Objective: Students will understand how a timeline helps communicare a sequence of events.

Summary

This timeline infographic takes readers step by step through key events in the history of voting rights in the United States.

Before Reading

Big Question: Remind students of the Big Question: Why is the right to vote important? Ask: How do you think a text about voting equality will help you think about the Big Question?

Preview Vocabulary

Discuss with students the words democracy and equal. Say: In a democracy, the citizens vote for leaders and laws. A true democracy is when everyone has equal voting rights. Who is allowed to vote in our democracy?

Oral Vocabulary The Path to Voting Equality features words that might be new or challenging to some students. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: Throughout history people have fought to ensure (make sure) that everyone is granted (gave) the same rights.

Word Web

Share a Word Web graphic organizer. To help students think about the relationships between words, guide them in creating a word web using the word vote. When they read The Path to Voting Equality, encourage them to add words to their web that relate to the word vote. A web for vote might look like this:

[a word web with vote in the center, and with rights, citizen, voice, and equal branching from it]

During Reading

ELEMENTS OF INFORMATIONAL TEXT

Text Structure The Path to Voting Equality is written with a chronological text structure. Say: Many history texts have a chronological structure, which means they tell about events in the order in which they happened in time. Why is this a helpful way to structure a text?

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. In the left column, write “Chronological Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the right column with what they know about chronological structure, and how it helps them understand what the writer is communicating. For example: “told in the order things happened”

Text Features Say: Timelines are a visual way of laying out information chronologically. Project or direct students to the timeline of The Path to Voting Equality. Say: Each block on the timeline is another event in the series. The events at the top happened first, and the events at the bottom happened last.

Return to the T-chart. In the left column write “Timelines.” Work with the class to fill in the right column with examples of how timelines help readers understand the text. For example: “information is shown in time order.”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read The Path to Voting Equality, have them review their completed word webs. Ask: Are there any more words that you would like to add?

Display these images from The Path to Voting Equality to help students as they think about words related to vote.

[images from the book for each word for the teacher to project]

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between The Path to Voting Equality and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • In The Path to Voting Equality, we learned how organized groups and individuals campaigned to ensure equal voting rights. What other groups have you read about who worked for people’s rights?
  • The Path to Voting Equality describes how having the right to vote doesn’t always mean that voting is possible. What other situations have you read about where the rules say one thing, but the reality is different?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, display the The Right to Vote: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Work with the class to fill in the row for The Path to Voting Equality with things they learned about voting rights.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: Why is the right to vote important? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • In The Path to Voting Equality we read about gradual change to voting laws. What role do you think individuals played in these changes?
  • When have you seen something unfair happening? Were you able to do something about it?

Write About The Big Question

Have students select one event from The Path to Voting Equality and write a few sentences about why they think it was an important step towards fair voting rules for everyone

Civil Rights at the Polls

Adapted from MyWorld
Informational Text - Word Count: 1493   Lexile: 1000L

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Illustrate historical and contemporary means of changing society.

  • Content Objective: Students will understand the evolution of voting rights for Black Americans.

  • Language Objective: Students will identify the individuals and organizations that brought about greater voting equality.

  • Text Structure Objective: Students will understand how a chronological text structure describes a series of events.

  • Text Feature Objective: Students will understand how section heads help guide a reader through a text.

Summary

This text describes the obstacles that were put in place to keep Black Americans from voting, and the people who fought against those and other unfair rules.

Before Viewing

Big Question: Remind students of the Big Question: Why is the right to vote important? Ask: How do you think Civil Rights at the Polls will help you think about the Big Question?

Preview Vocabulary

Discuss with students the words poll and protest. Ask: The word poll has a few different meanings, but in this text, it refers to voting. Maybe you have heard the term polling places. That means places where people go to vote.. Ask students if they have ever spoken out about something they disagreed with. Say: When you say that something is wrong, you are protesting. A protest can be one person disagreeing with someone, or it can be a larger group of people organized to speak out about an issue.

Oral Vocabulary Civil Rights at the Polls features words that might be new or challenging to some students. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: Black Americans campaigned to have the same opportunities as white Americans. It was a hard struggle, partly because the courts often ruled that unjust laws were fair.

Word Web

The History of Voting Rights

by Thomas Taylor
Informational Text - Word Count: 432   Lexile: 510L

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Illustrate historical and contemporary means of changing society.

  • Content Objective Students will understand the evolution of voting rights in the United States.

  • Language Objective Students will identify five stages of advancement towards voting quality.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a chronological text structure describes a series of events.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how pictures aid viewer understanding.

Summary

This video gives an overview of voting rights in America by breaking advances into five stages. It then goes into greater detail about efforts to put an end to unfair voting rules that targeted Black voters.

Before Reading

Big Question

Remind students of the Big Question: Why is the right to vote important? Ask: How do you think The History of Voting Rights will help you think about the Big Question?

Preview Vocabulary

Discuss with students the words amendment and suffrage. Ask: When you amend something, you fix it, or make it better. Amendments to the Constitution are meant to improve it. For example, some amendments grant specific people the right to vote. Ask students if they have heard of the suffragist movement. Say: Suffrage means “the right to vote.” The campaign to grant women the right to vote is referred to as the suffragist movement, but suffrage actually refers to any group’s voting rights.

Oral Vocabulary

The History of Voting Rights features words that might be new or challenging to some students. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: Since the Constitution did not ensure the right to vote for everyone, many groups had long struggles before they were granted the right to vote.

Word Web

Share a Word Web graphic organizer. To help students think about the relationships between words, guide them in creating a word web using the word amendment. When they read The History of Voting Rights, encourage them to add words to their web that relate to the word amendment. A web for amendment might look like this:

[a word web with amendment in the center, and with democracy, unfair, and equality branching from it]

During Reading

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure The History of Voting Rights has a chronological text structure. Say: Although part of the video goes back to talk more about stage four, most of the video has a chronological structure, organizing information in time order. Share with students a timeline graphic organizer. As they watch the video, have them note important people and events with their dates in order on the timeline.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. In the left column, write “Chronological Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the right column with what they know about chronological structure, and how it helps them understand what the narrator is communicating. For example: “information given in the order it happened”

Text Features Say: Pictures can help explain an idea. In a video, images shown as the narrator speaks help to explain what the narrator is describing. Project or direct students to 0:41 in the video. Ask: How does this photograph help you understand what the narrator is talking about?

Return to the T-chart. In the left column write “Pictures.” Work with the class to fill in the right column with examples of how pictures help readers understand the text. For example: “show examples of what is being talked about.”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read The History of Voting Rights, have them review their completed word webs. Ask: Are there any more words that you would like to add?

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between The History of Voting Rights and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • In The History of Voting Rights, we learned about the series of major changes to voting rights for different groups in America. Which of these groups have you read about in other texts? What have you learned about those groups in other texts you have read?
  • What more did you learn about those groups from watching The History of Voting Rights?
  • Think back to what you read about the voting rights in The Path to Voting Equality. What more did you learn about voting rights from viewing The History of Voting Rights??

Track the Texts After the discussion, display theThe Right to Vote: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Work with the class to fill in the row for The History of Voting Rights with things they learned about the right to vote.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around the Big Question: Why is the right to vote important? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • Who are some of the people in the video who emerged as leaders in the struggle to ensure voting rights for everyone? Why are they singled out as leaders?
  • Why do you think it can be so hard to create change, either as an individual or as a group?

Write About the Big Question

Ask students to write a journal entry from the point of view of someone who has gained the right to vote during one of the five stages described in the video. Their entries should express how they felt when they were denied the right to vote, and how they feel now that the right has finally been granted to them.

The Right to Vote

Wrap It Up

Now that the class has read some or all of the texts in this set, it is time to pull together all the information they have learned from their reading in order to explore and share their evolving understanding of the Big Question.

Vocabulary Round-Up

Word Sort Have students review the word webs they have created for the texts they have read, and then do a word sort, identifying new connections between the words using all the information they now have from their reading. For example, students might connect the words democracy, equality, ensure, and opportunity as they're all related to the goals of voting rights.

Ask students to share their word sorts and explain why they connected the words the way they did. Students may sort words based on their own experiences, so accept all reasonable explanations for the connections they make.

Discuss the Text Set's Big Question

Guide a class discussion around the Big Question: Why is the right to vote important? Encourage students to bring in knowledge they have learned from all the texts they have read in the text set, using as many of the words from their word webs as they can.

  • What did you already know about voting rights before you began reading this text set?
  • Now that you’ve read a lot of texts about voting rights, what new things did you learn?
  • What about voting rights would you like to learn more about?
  • Which texts were most useful in helping you answer the Big Question?

Culminating Project

Have students apply what they’ve learned from the text set to demonstrate and enhance their understanding. Review the Project Rubric with students, explaining in detail the expectations for the project.

PROJECT: A Campaign for Change!

Working alone or in groups, have students choose an issue that is important to them, one that is currently being debated or is up for a vote in your community. They might choose a new law that is being debated, or an issue of public policy. For example, a proposal for a new park or new school, or school uniforms.

Have them come up with a plan for how they will make people aware of the issue and encourage the most people to vote. Their campaigns should include catchy slogans, posters and signs, and plans for where and when to peacefully demonstrate to spread the word.

Students can refer to what they wrote in Write About the Big Question for each text to aid their thinking and planning. They may also use the Project Template to help them organize and clarify their thinking as they plan their campaigns. Encourage them to use the vocabulary they’ve learned from the texts in their campaigns.

Discussion Tips

An important part of building a vibrant classroom culture is creating an atmosphere in which all students feel empowered to participate in powerful classroom conversation. You may have noticed that your students who are naturally extroverted often participate freely in a whole-class discussion, while those who are more introverted or, perhaps, who come from cultures that respect different classroom norms, may need encouragement to share. You can use sentence prompts like these to invite all students into the conversation:

“That’s so interesting … please tell me more!”

“Can someone explain that in a different way?”

“What are some examples of that?”

“Can someone add on to that idea?”

Sharing and discussing ideas will often involve agreeing and/or disagreeing with what others say. As students listen carefully and respond thoughtfully to their classmates’ ideas, encourage them to express themselves in respectful and constructive ways. You may wish to post these sentence starters prominently, so students can refer to them often:

“I agree, and I also think fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I mostly agree, but fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I like what you just said because fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I respectfully disagree because fill in the blank line__________ .”
